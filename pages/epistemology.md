## What is Epistemology, and Why is it Important?

Epistemology refers to a branch of philosophy which studies knowledge. It generally deals with more meta-level questions, such as what knowledge is and what the sources of knowledge are. This page will not deal with extremely foundational questions, such as definining knowledge or whether any beliefs are truly justified. While these are interesting questions, they are not particularly important for understanding political theory or the social sciences in general. Rather, this page will be dedicated to addressing how to understand what is true or false in a general social scientific context.

It is first important to discuss the "is-ought distinction". "Is" claims, also often referred to as "positive" or "descriptive" claims, refer to claims about how the world is. For example, a descriptive claim might be "you are reading this sentence". It may be true, or it may be false, and it refers to something about reality. On the other hand, "ought" claims, also often referred to as "normative" claims, refer to claims about how the world ought to be. For example, a descriptive claim might be "you should be reading this sentence". Such a claim cannot be true or false in the same way that a descriptive claim is, as it addresses values as opposed to claiming something about reality.

This is not to say that there is no way in which descriptive and normative claims could be linked with one another. Take the sentence "the book 'The Great Gatsby' by F. Scott Fitzgerald is a good book". What does it mean for a book to be "good"? There is certainly some level of subjectivity to it, as some people may enjoy the book while others may not. However, every person's judgement of whether "The Great Gatsby" is a good book would imply an underlying value structure according to which books can be seen as "good" or "not good", even if not explicitly articulated or understood, as well as an assessment over how "The Great Gatsby" stacks up according to that value structure. The value structure is necessarily normative in nature, as it isn't something which "exists" in reality in the same way that a physical object does. However, given the value structure, assessing whether the book holds up to it is a descriptive claim, as it assesses the descriptive contents of the book itself. Thus, normative claims, when being evaluated, often require an assessment of both descriptive and normative claims.

Epistemology is the foundational means by which people can assess whether descriptive claims are true, and it is important in that it allows us to act in the world and benefit from our actions. Every person develops a form of epistemology, each of which has differential effects on how a person would interpret and engage with the world. The stages of development as outlined by Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development are indicative of how different epistemologies can have a siginificant impact on the way that a person can engage with the world, leaving aside criticisms of the theory, such as the assumption of a smooth, universal, and predictable path of development for children<ref>https://www.verywellmind.com/support-and-criticism-of-piagets-stage-theory-2795460</ref>. For example, the first stage as identified by Piaget, the sensorimotor stage which occurs at around the ages of 0 and 2, suggests that people do not innately have a sense of object permanence<ref>https://www.verywellmind.com/piagets-stages-of-cognitive-development-2795457</ref>. Object permanence refers to the notion that an object continues to exist even when it is not being directly observed. This is not necessarily a problem for infants, who are continously being supervised and cared for by adults, have a set of innate reflexes and responses that are adaptive for their period of development, and lack any strong motor skills which could put them at high risk of hurting themselves. However, an adult person without object permanence would struggle significantly with navigating things in their day-to-day life. As a simple example, they would be at much greater risk of tripping over objects, as they would assume that the object disappeared the second that they looked away from it. A person's epistemology is thus foundational to how they survive and engage in activities.

This is a relatively abstract and foundational example, but all of this is extremely relevant in the context of the social sciences. Many claims about policy rest in part upon empirical claims about what is the case. For example, take the claim "we should set the United States' national minimum wage to $15 an hour". This claim is often justified upon a basis that it would improve the well-being of those who would work minimum wage jobs. There can be debate about whether that is a value worth striving for, but assuming that it is, the question then becomes to what extent a $15/hour minimum wage actually improves the well-being of workers. If the minimum wage rate significantly reduces employment in sectors affected, then this might on net harm those who would work minimum wage jobs, as many of them become unemployed as a consequence of the policy change.

Many claims which revolve around humanities subjects, and deal with what ought to be the case, depend heavily on what is the case descriptively speaking. This is not to say that moral philosophy isn't relevant or doesn't play an important role in policy discussions, but policy discussions cannot be reduced to moralizing. In order to understand what is the case, especially in contentious or ambiguous situations as the social sciences often are, it is crucial to have a strong epistemology. This involves coming up with ways to answer questions about what is the case descriptively.

People are quite malleable in their views about political subjects. Despite political orientation having a substantial genetic component to it (see [Are Beliefs Genetic?](https://prometheandissident.github.io/psychology/#are-beliefs-genetic?)), people's political beliefs change all of the time. On political subjects where the truth is ambiguous, people are more likely to resort to mental heuristics, and it can be signficantly easier to get people to believe particular things with the correct priming and guiding of intuition. Depending on how it is framed, practically any set of descriptive claims an ideology professes can be made to seem intuitively true. A strong epistemology helps defend oneself against this extreme malleability, and ensure that when one's beliefs are changed, it is in accordance with the revelation of new information or ideas as opposed to an arbitrary sway towards a set of ideas that was merely sold better.

## On Scientific Reasoning

Science refers to a systematic process by which knowledge about the world is acquired. At its core is the "scientific method" which involves the continuous formation and testing of various hypotheses. This method is foundational to how information about areas such as chemistry and physics is acquired. However, its applicability in the context of politics and to claims about humans is often contested. Science is often split into the "hard sciences" and the "soft sciences", with the hard sciences including chemistry and physics and the soft sciences including areas such as sociology and economics, with biology somewhere in between.

Some argue that humans cannot be scientifically studied, and thus there can be no such thing as the "social sciences". There is no good reason why humans are excempt from this process. Contrary to what many might believe, human action is not completely random, and can be explained and even predicted by an understanding of the biological processes that underpin it (see [On Biological Determinism](https://prometheandissident.github.io/psychology/#on-biological-determinism)). Humans are made up of physical matter which adheres to the same laws of physics as matter which humans are not comprised of, and there is no evidence to suggest that there is any external thing interfacing with humans to determine their behavior outside of the physical matter that makes up people. There is no "ghost in the machine". Rather, human behavior is a complex interaction of chemicals and particles interfacing between the brain, the human's other physical parts, and its broader environment.

Certainly, human behavior is much more complex and difficult to predict than, for example, aspects of physics. However, the fact that it is complex and difficult to predict does not mean that scientific principles cannot in theory be applied, only that their application would present greater difficulty.
