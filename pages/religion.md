## On the Descriptive Validity of Relgion

For the purposes of this article, I will define religion in a more narrow way than I have in various sections of my page on [Psychology](https://prometheandissident.github.io/psychology/), most notably in [Is Leftism a Religion?](https://prometheandissident.github.io/psychology/#is-leftism-a-religion?). In this article, I will define religion as an organized set of beliefs surrounding the supernatural, particularly with a belief in the existence of a god or gods at the center. Religions generally speaking come with a set of rituals and cultural norms which are bundled into a "package" of sorts, often with vast doctrines which directly come from or are inspired by a "religious" text of sorts. In the case of the Christians, this takes the form of the Bible, with Muslims it takes the form of the Quran, with Hinduism it's the Bhagavad Gita, and so on.

I hold that religions are generally speaking false and do not correspond to reality, and hence that religious belief is in a sense, irrational. I will discuss particular reasons for believing this later on, but I want to make my position clear when it comes to analyzing my views on religion as a cultural practice. As discussed in [The Sociobiology of Culture](https://prometheandissident.github.io/culture/#the-sociobiology-of-culture), culture serves a number of adaptive purposes for providing the community with cohesion and a set of norms which can help them survive. Religion should be viewed as something very similar, and while there are irrational parts of it, that doesn't mean that it cannot be adaptive. If the irrational beliefs are relatively costless and provide cohesive benefits, the benefits may outweigh the costs. Furthermore, if religions create dogmas which, while not rationally articulated, have good reasons for existing for a survival purpose, such as dietary restrictions, then other aspects of religion can directly aid survival beyond merely providing social cohesion. I thus want to take religion seriously as a cultural phenomena, even if I disagree with the major world religions and believe that they're false.

I don't think that it is possible to know whether a god exists or not. I don't think that there is strong evidence for supernatural phenomena being able to explain things which have happened in the scope of human history, but it is possible that the start of the universe had supernatural origins. While I subscribe to atheism, I hold that both an atheistic view as well as a deistic view (i.e. a supreme being created the universe but does not intervene in its ongoing affairs) are both rational possibilities to hold. I regard many of the supernatural prescriptions made about, for example, the existence of free will or of souls independent from a person's body as irrational as well. These criterion disqualify the major world religions as viewpoints I would consider rational/reasonable. I do not believe that they are likely to be true.

## On Morality and Religion

A big topic which is regularly brought up in religious circles is the relationship between morality and religion. There is a view which suggests that in the absence of the existence of a god, there is no grounding for an objective morality, and morality merely reduces to people's preferences. As such, morality can only be subjective and arbitrary in nature in the absence of a god.

I would subscribe to a moral non-cognitivist view, whereby morality is only the expression of preferences, and says nothing about what is true or false in the world. I view the objective-subjective morality debate as uninteresting, as it comes across as people pre-emptively trying to get others to agree with or adhere to their morality through mere wordplay. Rather than being a thing which is etched into the world, morality is an emergent consequence of humanity's need to act in social groups in order to survive (see [On Morality and Human Sociality](https://prometheandissident.github.io/psychology/#on-morality-and-human-sociality)). Morality only makes sense insofar as other people exist in a society who interact with one another, and is fundamentally negotiative in nature. Morality emerges due to common interests and it being from a game theoretic view in everyone's best interest to adhere to a set of norms. However, these norms/moral principles only matter insofar as they have teeth, or enforcement in some way. This enforcement must come in the form of a risk to one's survival interests, whether they be imposed by other members of or the rest of the group in the form of a punishment of some kind, minor or major, or through the natural negative consequences of an action carrying forth.

Given that morality is merely about enforcement, in a sense the presence of an all-powerful, all-knowing god would be able to establish a type of "objective" morality, in that there would be one uniform set of standards which everyone is functionally incentivized to adhere to. Even then, god's morality would only matter insofar as he enforced it in such a way that dissuaded people from violating it. If a god is enforcing morality on threat of eternal damnation following a person dying, while this may dissuade most people from committing "immoral" acts, it wouldn't dissuade people with an extremely high time preference or extremely short-sighted preferences. Functionally speaking however, there effectively would be one standard which everyone is heavily incentivized to follow.

In the absence of a god, it's much more difficult to enforce morality, as there is no threat of use of force by a god, or of eternal damnation as exists in many religions. In some sense, the gods of many religions were likely created for that precise purpose - to try and enforce a standard of morality through giving people a new set of information about the costs of defecting from that standard. However, this does not mean that it's impossible, or that therefore religion is true. A belief can be adaptive in a particular context without being true, after all.

## The Validity of Present-Day Religions

This section will overview a number of arguments for why, inductively speaking, present-day religions are likely false. A full comprehensive examination of every religion and every aspect of each religion is far beyond the scope of this article, but this will combine a number of ideas which lead me to the conclusion that present-day religions were most likely man-made and are false.

### Religious Texts

Religious texts regularly say pseudoscientific things which are easily disproven. A short list:

- The Bible describes the earth as being created in seven days, with an ordering of events which wouldn't make sense for what was created. For example, the book of Genesis says that god created vegetation on the third day, but the day and night cycle with the sun and moon on the fourth day. Plants cannot survive in the absence of the sun, meaning that god did not provide plants with what they need to grow prior to creating them.
- Furthermore, the Bible strongly suggests that the earth is 6,000 years old, which contradicts virtually all evidence on the subject. There are even clonal trees which are older than 6,000 years<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_oldest_trees</ref>.
- Genesis also describes all living organisms as being popped into existence through divine creation, when we know that organisms evolved into their present states over millions of years and all share a common ancestor from billions of years ago.
- The existence of nephilism are also implied in Biblical texts, when there is no evidence for their existence
- Noah's flood is another event which is discussed in the bible, with no evidence of its existence.

Adherents of religion have, over time, said fewer erroneous things about the world. However, this is only through a progressive improvement of knowledge over time, which sometimes happened in opposition to religious doctrine and to the resistence of many religious adherents. Religious texts which are, in part or in whole, "divinely inspired" have practically never contributed new knowledge about science or medicine, and may have actually held some of it back to a degree through promoting false ideas about the world.
