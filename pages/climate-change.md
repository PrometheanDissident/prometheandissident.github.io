**<u>Summary</u>**

This section discusses climate change as a problem, focusing primarily on pollution caused by CO2. I argue that the damages from climate change are likely to be substantial, although not apocalyptic, and that the most effective strategy involves a combination of passive techniques of reducing non-renewable subsidies and degregulating renewable industries, as well as stronger investment in renewable energy research and development. In addition, a carbon tax which accounts for the potential distributional consequences can function as an effective tool to offset some emissions, particularly when it pays for itself, although the focus should be primarily on actively making renewable energy sources more attractive rather than making non-renewable energy sources less attractive. Emphasis should especially be put on solutions which cost less than the long-term benefits they will provide in terms of offsetting climatic damages or other benefits. This type of policy framework, which recognizes that climate change is something to focus on but is not the be-all-end-all issue, appears to be the most reasonable way of addressing the issues.

## Understanding Climate Change

Climate change refers to the trend of the rising average global temperature, which has begun around the beginning of the 19th century, and its associated environmental changes<ref>https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_WGI_SPM_final.pdf</ref>. Since the industrial revolution, Earth's average surface air temperature has increased by nearly 1.5degrees celsius, and it is expected to increase further. On a decadal level, earth's temperature has risen by an average of 0.08° Celsius per decade since 1880. Since 1981, the rate of warming has doubled to 0.18° C per decade. The 10 warmest years in the historical record have all occurred since 2010 as of an article written in 2024<ref>Climate change: global temperature. (2024, January 18). NOAA Climate.gov. https://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/climate-change-global-temperature</ref>.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Global_Temperature_And_Forces_With_Fahrenheit.svg/1280px-Global_Temperature_And_Forces_With_Fahrenheit.svg.png" alt="Global Average Temperatures" width="500">

The fact that the global average temperature is rising is generally not disputed. While the precise relative influences of human activity or other things on the recent warming trends is not clear, there is an overwhelming scientific and organizational consensus affirming the legitimacy of the anthropogenic global warming thesis, the idea that the temperature of the earth is increasing and that this is heavily caused by humans<ref>Scientific consensus - NASA Science. (n.d.). https://science.nasa.gov/climate-change/scientific-consensus/</ref><ref>California, S. O. (n.d.). List of worldwide scientific organizations - Office of Planning and Research. https://www.opr.ca.gov/facts/list-of-scientific-organizations.html</ref><ref>Anderegg, W. R. L., Prall, J. W., Harold, J., & Schneider, S. H. (2010). Expert credibility in climate change. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 107(27), 12107–12109. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1003187107</ref><ref>Cook, J., Nuccitelli, D., Green, S. A., Richardson, M., Winkler, B., Painting, R., Way, R., Jacobs, P., & Skuce, A. (2013). Quantifying the consensus on anthropogenic global warming in the scientific literature. Environmental Research Letters, 8(2), 024024. https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/8/2/024024</ref><ref>IPCC reflects scientific consensus on climate change. (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/Hulme-IPCC-consensus.htm</ref><ref>The Cook et al. (2013) 97% consensus result is robust. (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/97-percent-consensus-robust.htm</ref><ref>Powell, J. (2017). Scientists reach 100% consensus on anthropogenic global warming. Bulletin of Science Technology & Society, 37(4), 183–184. https://doi.org/10.1177/0270467619886266</ref>.

In addition, the anthropogenic global warming thesis has been widely affirmed by the fact that climate models, which assume the thesis is true, show high predictive validity, suggesting that the thesis provides the best explanatory power for understanding the causes of climate change. While it is the case that short-term weather forecasts are bad at predicting future weather events and temperatures, climate models which look in the long-term have been as a whole very accurate at measuring temperature trends over time<ref>IPCC global surface warming projections have been accurate. (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/ipcc-global-warming-projections.htm</ref><ref>Hausfather, Z., Drake, H. F., Abbott, T., & Schmidt, G. A. (2020). Evaluating the performance of past climate model projections. Geophysical Research Letters, 47(1). https://doi.org/10.1029/2019gl085378</ref>. The reason why weather can't really be predicted with current modelling while climate can is because climate models take the average of a number of weather events over time and use that to predict mean temperature trends<ref>How reliable are climate models? (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/climate-models.htm</ref>. This is similar to how a person can't with certainty predict the outcome of a coin toss but can predict the number of heads and tails of a coin toss over a large number of trials with a fair degree of certainty. Moreover, it should be noted that, at least regarding the IPCC predictions, sea level rise and arctic sea ice models have underestimated the climate response, as they failed to take into account positive feedback loops that exist with such phenomena.

The relative predictive accuracy of climate modelling suggests that modelling of anthropocentric or non-anthropocentric consequences of climate change which rely on assumptions of a given climatic temperature change should not exhibit significant error, much less a systematic bias in a particular direction. Thus, this page will rely upon modelling and future predictions of the potential effects of cimate change as a way to assess its significance, how much mitigative efforts should be prioritized, and which mitigative efforts should be prioritized. Climate change has had a significant number of effects on the environment, both directly and indirectly, many of which potentially carry implications for human well-being. These will be discussed in turn as well as the ways in which this issue should (or should not) be dealt with.

## Effects of Climate Change

Climate change is widely known to have a number of effects on the environment itself, which are on net negative. Ignoring the effects climate change has on the human population, climate change contributes to a rapidly-changing environment and high variability in environmental conditions which leads to many species being harmed. This is done through its effects on various aspects of environments, and leads to a high rate of extinction for many species. Here are some of the major negative effects of climate change:

- **Biological -** Plants and animals cannot adapt to the rapidly changing climatic conditions that humans have caused<ref>Can animals and plants adapt to global warming? (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/Can-animals-and-plants-adapt-to-global-warming.htm</ref>. Warming is happening at a rate 20-60 times higher than natural ice age temperature changes. The extinction rate for species is 100-1000 times higher than normal. Even ignoring temperature changes, human civilizations cause migration, a common adaptive behavior to be a more difficult option, and the introduction of invasive species and deforestation also increase the vulnerability of species. Moreover, climatic changes are likely to be too fast for humans to adapt. Currently, 1% of the earth's land is considered in barely livable heat, with most of it in the Sahara and other hot desert regions. By 2070, with unmitigated CO2 emissions, this could jump up to 19%. Parts of the US could have a month's worth of dangerously hot "wet bulb" temperatures by 2050, which prevent the body from cooling off through sweating.
- **Droughts -** Droughts have increased in frequency worldwide and have become more intense<ref>Hille, K. B. (2023, September 29). NASA finds drought in eastern Mediterranean worst of past 900 years - NASA. NASA. https://www.nasa.gov/technology/nasa-finds-drought-in-eastern-mediterranean-worst-of-past-900-years/</ref><ref>Marvel, K., Cook, B. I., Bonfils, C. J. W., Durack, P. J., Smerdon, J. E., & Williams, A. P. (2019). Twentieth-century hydroclimate changes consistent with human influence. Nature, 569(7754), 59–65. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1149-8</ref>. Projections indicate that these will likely increase over time<ref>NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies, Ocean and Climate Physics, Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory of Columbia University, & Earth and Atmospheric Sciences, Cornell University. (2015). Unprecedented 21st century drought risk in the American Southwest and Central Plains. Sci. Adv., 1, e1400082. https://www.science.org/doi/pdf/10.1126/sciadv.1400082</ref>. Although these projections look at one region of the United States, it seems reasonable to assert similar trends will likely occur in other places given the increased droughts worldwide. Increases in droughts that have already occurred have likely caused or contributed to conflict in already unstable regions, with Kelley et al. (2014) finding that the 2007-2010 drought contributed to the conflict in Syria<ref>Kelley, C. P., Mohtadi, S., Cane, M. A., Seager, R., & Kushnir, Y. (2015). Climate change in the Fertile Crescent and implications of the recent Syrian drought. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 112(11), 3241–3246. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1421533112</ref>.
- **Wildfires -** Climate change increases the risk of wildfires occurring<ref>Climate change increases the risk of wildfires confirms new review. (2020, January 20). ScienceDaily. https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2020/01/200114074046.htm</ref>. Fire weather patterns have increased on 25% of the earth's vegetated surface, and a 20% increase in the global mean length of the fire season. While humans cannot stop high-risk fire weather from occurring, through land management decisions and ignition sources, they can significantly decrease the harm of wildfires and the probability that they will sprout up. In the rocky mountains, there has been significant decreases in the level of tree regeneration in the 21st century, due to decreased moisture<ref>Stevens‐Rumann, C. S., Kemp, K. B., Higuera, P. E., Harvey, B. J., Rother, M. T., Donato, D. C., Morgan, P., & Veblen, T. T. (2017). Evidence for declining forest resilience to wildfires under climate change. Ecology Letters, 21(2), 243–252. https://doi.org/10.1111/ele.12889</ref>.
- **Ocean -** Between 1900 and 2015 the sea level has risen an average 8 inches. Currently, it is rising at about an eighth of an inch per year, although this is expected to exponentially grow in the years to come. By 2100, it is projected that sea level will rise another 1-8 feet<ref>How is sea level rise related to climate change? (n.d.). https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/sealevelclimate.html</ref>. Even under the 1.5 degrees scenario, sea level rise is still expected to go over several meters over the next thousand years. The 2 degrees threshold would result in a 9-meter sea level rise. Economic costs in the world's largest coastal cities due to flooding in 2005 were $6 billion, in the year of Hurricane Katrina. This is expected to jump up to $1 trillion by 2050<ref>Reducing carbon emissions will limit sea level rise. (2018, July 18). ScienceDaily. https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2018/07/180716130724.htm</ref>.

Many of these effects of climate change are likely to have a direct impact on human activity. However, I do think that even if humans weren't directly harmed by these activities, there still would be a case for caring about climate change to an extent. Biodiversity is something which is beautiful, and people generally speaking recognize its beauty, which is why nature parks are so popular. Preserving that biodiversity is thus something which is valuable to a degree in itself. How that is weighed against other values is a different question, but the point is that climate change would still be regarded as on net harmful even if it did not directly harm human lives or activities.

## How Serious?

While much of what has been discussed primarily deals with non-anthropocentric impacts of climate change, there are some impacts which do directly affect humans, including economic, health, and social stability impacts. It is important to quantify these to see roughly speaking how bad climate change will be for us and how much we should care about it/act to mitigate it.

- **Disease -** Climate change will lead to an increase in the spread of a variety of diseases<ref>Towers, S., Chowell, G., Hameed, R., Jastrebski, M., Khan, M., Meeks, J., Mubayi, A., & Harris, G. (2013). Climate change and influenza: the likelihood of early and severe influenza seasons following warmer than average winters. PLoS Currents. https://doi.org/10.1371/currents.flu.3679b56a3a5313dc7c043fb944c6f138</ref><ref>Ryan, S. J., Carlson, C. J., Mordecai, E. A., & Johnson, L. R. (2019). Global expansion and redistribution of Aedes-borne virus transmission risk with climate change. PLoS Neglected Tropical Diseases, 13(3), e0007213. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pntd.0007213</ref>.
- **Economic -** Climate damages are estimated to be between 2.5-7.5% of GDP in 2050, and already over the past few decades have been found to cost a few percent of global GDP annually. Investment into mitigation will likely rise in the short to medium term, but this will be unproductive investment as it is replacing existing capital<ref>Nixon, J. & Oxford Economics. (2019). THE ECONOMIC IMPACT OF GLOBAL WARMING. https://www.cheshirescouts.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/The-economic-impact-of-global-warming-Oxford-Economics.pdf</ref>. However, the benefits of adaptation policies may end up increasing GDP and paying for themselves<ref>The Economic Consequences of Climate Change | READ online. (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/too-expensive-intermediate.htm</ref>.
- **Health -** Air pollution kills an estimated 7 million people every year<ref>World Health Organization: WHO. (2019, July 30). Air pollution. https://www.who.int/health-topics/air-pollution#tab=tab_1</ref>. Death rates from air pollution worldwide have been decreasing since 1990<ref>https://assets.ourworldindata.org/grapher/exports/death-rate-by-source-from-air-pollution.svg</ref>. An emissions reduction to a 2 degrees scenario would cause an estimated 153 million fewer premature deaths, with about 40% occurring over the next 40 years<ref>Shindell, D., Faluvegi, G., Seltzer, K., & Shindell, C. The impact of climate change in 2100 will be comparable to leading causes of death such as cancer and infectious disease (this was before COVID existed)<ref>https://bfi.uchicago.edu/wp-content/uploads/BFI_WP_201851.pdf</ref>. Vohra et al. (2021) estimates a global total of 10.2 million premature deaths annually from fossil fuel combustion<ref>Vohra, K., Vodonos, A., Schwartz, J., Marais, E. A., Sulprizio, M. Reducing greenhouse gas emission can help reduce deaths associated with air pollution<ref>UNC Gillings School of Global Public Health. (2017, October 31). - **Conflict/Stability -** Each standard deviation in temperature increases interpersonal conflict by 2.4% and intergroup conflict by 11.3%<ref>Burke, M., Hsiang, S. M., & Miguel, E. (2015). So there are moderate negative impacts of climate change. It should be noted that these are likely to be permanent or effectively permanent in some cases given that some climatic variables have global thresholds which if crossed will have qualitatively negative material effects for people, such as ocean acidification, climate change, and potentially the phosphorous cycle and stratospheric ozone depletion. However, some others don't including land-use change, biodiversity loss, nitrogen cycle, global freshwater use, atmospheric aerosol loading, and chemical pollution<ref>The Planetary Boundaries Hypothesis: A Review of the evidence. Thus, there is a strong case to be made that while climate change is not likely to be world-ending, it is something which we should be aware of. In cases where the costs of mitigation are little, climate mitigation strategies should be pursued, and in cases where the costs of mitigation are high, we should try to adapt to climate change in whatever ways we can.

## The Ethical Dilemmas of Climate Change

Climate change is a very difficult issue to politically address. The primary reason for this is because it is international in nature. No one country can act to "stop" climate change, as all of the driver of it, most notably greenhouse gas emissions, are things which all countries engage in. One country may individually end their emissions, but this will not stop the vast majority of emissions from still occurring. As a result, climate change requires multiple countries to act in unison to be addressed. This is difficult, as climate change constitutes a type of "prisoner's dilemma" problem. This occurs because if every country cooperates to reduce their emissions, every country is in theory better off than in the status quo (assuming it's done with relatively little harm to the countries). However, each country, individually speaking, would be even better off if they "free rode" off of other countries reducing their emissions without reducing their own, as they could incur practically all of the benefits of little climate change while not incurring any of the costs. Because every country faces this incentive, the resultant Nash equlibrium is that no country is willing to reduce their emissions.

The way to solve prisoner's dilemma-type games is to force players to make decisions. This can work if the prisoner's dilemma situation involves several individuals within a state, as the state has a monopoly on the use of force and can use that to force individuals to play particular actions, which ultimately benefits everyone. However, there is no one "world government" which can force the actions of countries to the degree which would be required to do this in the case of climate change. As a result, it is very difficult for binding agreements to be made on climate change, as even if countries on paper agree to a particular goal, they are incentivized not to follow through and to free ride on other countries.

A second, although less serious, ethical dilemma is also present in climate change, which concerns the distributional effects that mitigation strategies present. A significant proportion of emissions come from countries which are not especially high income. A 2024 report found that high-income countries were responsible for just 29 percent of GHG emissions in 2022<ref>https://www.cgdev.org/blog/climate-and-development-three-charts-update</ref>, indicating that the majority of emissions are coming from countries which are not especially rich, and may be underdeveloped in some cases. It is the case that nonetheless on a per-capita basis high-income countries are emiting more, but the fact remains that any serious mitigation strategy will require some sacrifices to be made by countries which are not especially rich. This begs the question as to what extent these countries should be expected to commit to any climate mitigative strategies if they end up becoming costly.

## Mitigation Strategies

There are a number of possible mitigative strategies which can be undertaken to address climate change, some of which are much more invasive in nature than others. Many of these strategies, however, have the potential to be non-invasive, beneficial in other areas, and agreeable to the general population, while also addressing climate change to an extent. Some of the more passive strategies:

- **Getting rid of environmentally damaging subsidies -** Many countries today have significant subsidies on fossil fuels, often as a result of rent-seeking behavior (see [Corrupting Influence of Capitalism?](https://prometheandissident.github.io/economics/#corrupting-influence-of-capitalism?)). These help artificially reduce the price of fossil fuels relative to renewables, leading to consumers becoming more reliant on fossil fuels relative to renewable energy sources. The subsidies are not only economically distortionary, but also lead to greater damage to the environment. As such, getting rid of them would likely both benefit the economy while also addressing climatic issues. This should especially be considered prior to one considering engaging in carbon taxation, as a carbon tax while also subsidizing fossil fuels would lead to more money going through the government when we can have the same effect without engaging in as much government spending in the first place. Beyond direct subsidies for fossil fuels, agricultural subsidies are very common. Agricultural subsidies distort market prices, interfere with trade, adversely affect poor farmers in developing countries, burden US taxpayers, and lead to environmental degradation<ref>Ostria, M. (2013). How U.S. agricultural subsidies harm the environment, taxpayers and the poor. https://www.ncpathinktank.org/pdfs/ib126.pdf</ref>. As a result, these are another type of subsidy which should be gotten rid of in general.
- **Getting rid of regulations which hold back renewable energy development -** As discussed in [The Role of Regulation](https://prometheandissident.github.io/economics/#the-role-of-regulation), in general regulations are harmful to economic growth as well as income inequality. In most cases they are implemented, they do not meaningfully help the average person, and risk increasing market power within industries. Deregulation in renewable energy industries would allow for cheaper renewable energy, as well as more investment, research, and development done in the field, which will increase the speed at which renewable energies can be adopted in all aspects of the economy, and the speed at which the economy can transition away from non-renewable energy sources. As a result, deregulation of the renewable energy industry can be a cheap and passive way to help address climate change in the long-run.

One major passive way which many discuss to address climate change is through general economic development. This comes from the hypothesized "Environmental Kuznets Curve" (EKC), which states that there is an inverse u-shaped relationship between a country's level of economic development and its level of environmental degradation<ref>https://www.economicshelp.org/blog/14337/environment/environmental-kuznets-curve/</ref>. This is theorized to be the case because countries which are wealthier tend to be more able to care about the long-term, due to higher material prosperity, as well as their citizens having more free time on their hands, which means that they are more likely to support eco-friendly policies and behaviors. In practice, this does not appear to empirically bear out, at least in terms of CO2 emissions. The wealthiest countries today, as discussed previously, on a per-capita basis are those which emit the highest levels of CO2. While it is reasonable to suggest that some forms of pollution rise and fall with economic development, in a similar way to how the EKC hypothesis describes, these tend to be more localized pollution problems as opposed to the globalized nature of CO2 emissions. Therefore, the passive strategy often discussed of developing countries so that they can focus on climate change more is unlikely to succeed, and may, if anything, make the problem worse. This is not to suggest that we should advocate for degrowth or for poorer countries to stay poorer. It must always be remembered that climate change solutions are a balancing act, and environmental protection, while an important value, is not the only value we should have. It is to suggest, however, that growth as a passive solution is unlikely to bear much fruit.

There are also a set of more active policies which could potentially be implemented to address climate change. Unlike the more passive approaches, which come with few to no tradeoffs, these come with more serious tradeoffs and should be done with more careful consideration. Active approaches include the following:

- **Command-and-control regulations -** Command-and-control regulations refers to regulations which explicitly put a ban on particular activities which create pollution, or put a cap on them. This is the simplest active response a government can have towards climate change. Generally speaking, it is considered a suboptimal policy due to the restrictions it places on the actions of firms. Command-and-control regulations ignore the fact that firms value a marginal unit of pollution differently, with some firms valuing a marginal unit of pollution higher than others, due to the profit opportunities that it can present. In many cases, individuals may value a marginal unit of pollution differently as well. If that is used for a more valuable good or resource, then that marginal unit would be more valuable. The problem with command-and-control regulations is that while they can succeed in lowering pollution, they fail to effectively distribute pollution rights/abilities in a way which would maximize efficiency. For the same amount of pollution which could hypothetically occur, command-and-control regulations limit the level of economic activity to below what could in theory occur. Thus, command-and-control regulations lead to inefficiencies which are similar in nature to what occurs under centrally planned economies, hence the similarity in language and terminology used to describe both. Furthermore, the imposition of command-and-control environmental regulations, like regulations in general, leads to greater market power through limiting the ability for other firms to enter markets. While this can happen with some of the other approaches which will be discussed later, command-and-control regulations are especially prone to this occurring, which is why they tend to be preferred by firms which are affected by such policies<ref>https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1008782132515</ref>. A regulation which limits the entry of competitors can ultimately benefit a firm and raise their profits, rather than hindering them. Unfortunately, while these policies are some of the worst ways to deal with climate change, they are some of the most popular, due to voter ignorance over what is effective climate policymaking<ref>https://energyathaas.wordpress.com/2026/04/13/the-climate-policy-paradox-why-voters-reject-the-tools-that-economists-advocate-most/?trk=feed_main-feed-card_feed-article-content</ref>.
- **Cap and Trade -** Simple neoclassical microeconomic models of a particular market focus on the determination of an equilibrium quantity of a good and its price using supply and demand. Thus, the two dimensions which microeconomic models tend to view goods in terms of are its price and its quantity. Cap and trade systems, like command-and-control regulations, limit the quantity of pollution<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_emission_trading</ref>. However, they do so in a more market-friendly way which avoid some of the problems associated with command-and-control regulations. Specifically, while they place a "cap" on the amount of pollution allowed by firms in an economy, they do so by distributing "pollution permits" among firms in the economy. This allows firms to buy and sell the rights to pollution from each other. This can ensure that the same amout of pollution as would occur in a command-and-control regulatory system can occur, with the added benefit of ensuring that the permits are allocated to firms with the highest willingness to pay, or the firms who generally speaking derive the greatest benefit from polluting. It thus achieves the same pollution level outcome with a higher level of efficiency than would occur with command-and-control regulations. There is, however, a similar concern in cap and trade systems with regards to the possibility of market power emerging. The largest political question which arises with cap and trade systems is how the initial allocation of permits is to occur. Rent-seeking firms can take advantage of this problem of initial allocation of permits through ensuring that they have a higher proportion of starting permits than they otherwise would. This can lead to general economic inefficiency. Because the way permits are initially allocated is a question which cap and trade policies have to address, this is a significant risk to any cap and trade policy.
- **Carbon Taxation -** Whereas cap and trade limits the quantity of pollution directly, carbon taxation controls the price directly through the addition of a tax. The general theory behind carbon taxes is that there is some additional externality associated with pollution which is not borne out by the polluter, or a monetary cost. Various economists and studies have attempted to quantify this "cost", referring to it as the "social cost of carbon". The idea behind a carbon tax is that it raises the price of pollution to the level of the social cost of carbon, allowing revenue to be derived from the pollution as well as ensuring that pollution only occurs when the benefit associated with the pollution exceeds the social cost that it creates. One limitation of a carbon tax is that when there are market fluctuations in the price of carbon, the tax no longer reflects the social cost of carbon. As such, it is difficult to ensure that it truly reflects the social costs even when the social cost of carbon is "correctly" calculated. Furthermore, if one is attempting to use a tax to arrive at a particular level of societal pollution, while it is theoretically possible to calculate the price level such that in a particular market the equilibrium quantity of pollution is at a desired level, this similarly breaks when once the market fluctuates. Therefore, there are limitations conceptually to the existence of carbon taxes, although it has the advantage of not risking rent-seeking behavior in the same way that cap and trade and command-and-control regulation policies do. However, while carbon taxes do not have a firm-level distribution challenge, they do have a type of redistributive challenge of their own. Carbon taxes, being consumption taxes, tend to have regressive distributional consequences (see [The Tax Spread](https://prometheandissident.github.io/economics/#the-tax-spread)). Because poorer people tend to either directly consume gas when it is taxes, or consume products made through pollution, both as a disproportionate share of their income relative to wealthier people, the carbon tax disproportionately taxes the poor. This concern can be alleviated through policy decisions surrounding how the tax revenue should be spent. In theory, if all of the tax revenue was redistributed to the poor, the carbon tax could in theory have progressive distributive consequences<ref>https://taxpolicycenter.org/taxvox/distributional-effects-carbon-tax-depend-how-revenue-spent</ref>. This merely depends on policy design. Research does show that carbon taxes, which are a very common policy instrument used to address climate change, do lead to emission reductions. A meta-analysis which looked at evaluations of 21 pricing schemes found that across the schemes, there were emissions reductions of between 4% and 15% after correcting for publication bias in the research<ref>https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-48512-w?error=cookies_not_supported&code=2754bf5f-923f-4b5d-ae5c-ba73a7a1e19a</ref>.

Out of the active policy solutions laid out here, I would prefer the carbon taxation one first and foremost. This is not to say that this would be the first policy I would implement to address concerns about climate change, but it is preferable to cap and trade and command-and-control regulations. Another set of possible strategies concerns ways to remove carbon from the atmosphere which is already there. This can be done through the development of carbon capture technologies<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage</ref> or increased creation of carbon sinks<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_sink</ref>.

**On the Coase Theorem -** A very common concept which is used in discussions of environmental economics is the idea of the Coase theorem<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Problem_of_Social_Cost</ref>, which suggests that in the absence of transaction costs, firms and individuals will organically negotiate pollution disputes with one another in such a way that the cost of pollution is naturally accounted for in production of goods and services. The key challenge here is transaction costs, which refer to any cost associated with parties negotiating with one another. While the Coase theorem can work organically or through minor reforms in the case of localized pollution problems, with large-scale pollution problems such as CO2, there are millions or billions of polluters and millions of billions affected by pollution, which makes any negotiation virutally impossible irrespective of any potential future legal or technological developments. As a result, the Coase theorem cannot address climate change to any meaningful degree.

**On Capitalism as a Cause of Climate Change -** It is very common for people to assert that climate change is fundamentally a function of capitalistic greed, and that in the absence of capitalism, climate change would not be an issue. This is a misunderstanding of how well alternatives to capitalism work. While it is the case that externality problems which lead to climate change are fundamentally problems of self-interest, that does not mean that an alternative economic system would fare any better in terms of how it addresses climate change. In fact, socialistic systems likely in many cases lead to more environmental damage. When comparing Western Europe and Eastern Europe during the era of the Soviet Union, it was clear that socialist Eastern Europe was significantly less environmentally friendly. Most famously was the significant alteration of two bodies of water: the aral sea and lake baikal due to environmental damages<ref>https://www.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/serials/files/cato-journal/1992/11/cj12n2-2.pdf</ref>. In socialist systems, it is very easy to allow political considerations to dominate economic ones, often to the detriment of the environment. In many cases in the USSR, the overriding political imperative of industrial expansion outweighed any environmental considerations. There are also fewer incentives to prevent waste of resources such as energy, unlike in market systems where even in cases where there is pollution going on, there is a constraint against it which is ensuring that resources are used efficiently according to firm's profit margins. Because alternative, centrally planned systems do not appear to fare any better when it comes to addressing climate change, capitalism cannot be argued to be the primary cause of environmental issues, although technological development which happened under capitalism may be a more distant cause for many environmental problems.

### Investment in Renewables

Most of the solutions thus far have primarily dealt with making non-renewable energy sources actively look less attractive economically compared to renewable energy sources. But what about making renewable energy sources look more attractive? Ultimately, any means by which climate change is addressed which does not want to completely sacrifice any possibility of energy production must look to renewables as a source of energy to transition to. This transition can happen in a way which positively affects economic growth. Some research suggests that the total cost to implement mitigation efforts to essentially half estimated CO2 in 2030 is less than 1% of projected global GDP once taking into account investment which will pay for itself<ref>Ritchie, H., Rosado, P., Samborska, V., & Roser, M. (2024, April 15). Climate change. Our World in Data. https://ourworldindata.org/climate-change</ref>. Like most R&D, green R&D has the potential to significantly boost economic growth in a way which will ensure that it pays for itself (see [Maximizing Innovation and Technology](https://prometheandissident.github.io/economics/#maximizing-innovation-and-technology)). As such, it is a prime way in which the transition towards renewables should be achieved.

One big concern of energy transition towards renewable energy sources is that it will be disruptive to the jobs of people currently working in industries such as oil and gas. As discussed in [On Technological Progress](https://prometheandissident.github.io/economics/#on-technological-progress), all technological advancements create employment risks, where they significantly disrupt many industries which can jeopardize the employment of them. However, in the long-run, society generally benefits from technological development and net employment usually rises. The effects of energy policies and environmental regulation on employment are unclear, but they appear to have small but positive effects on employment<ref>Pestel, N. (2014). Employment effects of green energy policies. IZA World of Labor. https://doi.org/10.15185/izawol.76</ref><ref>Horbach, J. (2016). Impacts of regulation on eco-innovation and job creation. IZA World of Labor. https://doi.org/10.15185/izawol.265</ref><ref>Renewable energy creates more jobs than fossil fuels. (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/renewable-energy-investment-kills-jobs.htm</ref>. Additionally, they decrease air polluting, which increases health and labor productivity. Keep in mind that this is a net effect. As a transition occurs to more green energy policies and energy sources there are people in the fossil fuel industry who will become unemployed. These people's interests should be considered in any green energy policy through short-run retraining programs and long-run shifts in the educational system<ref>Wayback machine. (n.d.). https://web.archive.org/web/20240414044922/https://www.oecd.org/environment/Employment-Implications-of-Green-Growth-OECD-Report-G7-Environment-Ministers.pdf</ref>. Income support is also important.

There are numerous barriers to renewable energy usage in the United States. These include upfront capital costs, reliability misconceptions, an unequal playing field due to subsidies of fossil fuels, a well-established existing market, and problems with where to put decentralized plants and how the energy will be transmitted to power lines<ref>Barriers to renewable energy technologies. (2014, June 6). Union of Concerned Scientists. https://www.ucsusa.org/resources/barriers-renewable-energy-technologies#.W-s1fN-YVuR</ref>. Many of these problems can be resolved through implementing passive reforms as advocated for previously as well as increasing R&D spending to address mant of the technological concerns.

Some of the concerns with renewables, such as the problem of providing baseload and non-intermittent energy, can already be solved to a strong degree with present technology. Bioelectricity from burning crop and plantation forest residues and solar power with low-cost thermal storage can already provide baseload energy as good as fossil fuels. Intermittency can be solved through interconnecting power lines across geographical regions to account for individual variability, while coupling them with peak-load plants such as gas turbines fueled by biofuels or natural gas which can be quickly turned on to fill in gaps of low wind and solar production. Numerous regional and global case studies have outlined realistic plans which allow us to meet 100% of energy demand with renewable sources<ref>Can renewables provide baseload power? (n.d.). Skeptical Science. https://skepticalscience.com/renewable-energy-baseload-power-advanced.htm</ref>.

I argue that any transition towards renewable energy sources must be done with a wide range of sources, and there should not be stigma against using some sources due to unfounded fears that they could cause disasters. Contrary to the assertions of many, nuclear energy is incredibly safe. Statistically speaking, nuclear energy is one of the safest sources of energy in terms of its death rate from accidents and air pollution<ref>Ritchie, H., & Roser, M. (2024, March 20). What are the safest and cleanest sources of energy? Our World in Data. https://ourworldindata.org/safest-sources-of-energy</ref>.
