## Against contemporary progressivism

Contemporary progressivism, or the modern interpretation of progressivism, tends to come in two strands. One is a social progressivist strand, which views progress in terms of the expanding moral circle<ref>https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-019-12227-0</ref>, or as the sacralization of historically marginalized groups, including racial, gender, and sexual minorities<ref>https://erickaufmann.substack.com/p/early-sources-of-woke</ref>. This sacralization can also extend to disabled people, the homeless, the poor, or in some strands, even non-human animals. A second strand is a technological progressivist one. Rather than viewing progress in social terms, technological progressivism views progress in terms of the expansion and development of technology.

Embedded within both conceptions of contemporary progressivism is an implicit or explicit notion that 1) the future will inevitably move in the direction of said typology of progressivism; 2) that said typology of progressivism is intrinsically good; and 3) that said typology of progressivism involves the promotion of novelty in some way. However, under social progressivism, none of these three conditions actually apply, and technological progressivism, while fulfilling conditions 1 and 3, struggles to fulfill condition 2.

With regard to social progressivism, despite the use of implicit language and assertions that the ideology is on the “right side of history”, implying that social progressivism will inevitably win, the ideology fails to offer any substantive mechanism through which the expanding moral circle or increased sacralization of historically marginalized groups is in fact an inevitability. Thus, rather than the assertions constituting statements of fact or objective laws of progress, they are better understood as religious or rhetorical in nature.

Social progressivism is furthermore not inherently good. While an in-depth analysis of the ideology goes far beyond the scope of this text, there are numerous reasons to believe this to be the case. The epistemology of social progressivism places an excessive emphasis on the notion that human behavior is environmentally mediated, when in reality, it is a complex mixture of genetics and environment. Thus, inequalities which progressivism aims to reduce are, in many cases, partly or largely genetically mediated, which means that their reduction is in many cases not possible or desirable. Furthermore, social progressivism ignores the reality of tradeoffs between cultural diversity and group-level equality of outcomes, both of which are supported in the sacralization of minority groups through advocacy for causes such as diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives. In reality, due to the strong influence cultural diversity has in organically producing differential outcomes between groups, cultural diversity almost always necessitates inequity.

Finally, social progressivism is not intrinsically pro-novelty, as it advocates for restrictions on many novel innovations on the basis that they risk de-sacralizing certain groups. Examples include restrictions on controversial research, as shown through extensive instances of academics being mobbed or censured for their ideas<ref>https://psychrabble.medium.com/the-threat-to-academic-freedom-from-academics-4685b1705794</ref>; the rise of research ethics requirements, which while necessary in certain contexts, have effectively led to bans on conducting certain types of legitimate research<ref>https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-022-01443-2</ref>; and opposition to certain technologies on the basis that they are biased racially or in other ways, such as machine learning predictive policing algorithms<ref>https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/07/17/1005396/predictive-policing-algorithms-racist-dismantled-machine-learning-bias-criminal-justice/</ref>. In these respects, social progressivism may be anti-novelty, and may ironically exhibit anti-progressive qualities.

Technological progressivism performs better than social progressivism in substantively aligning with its implicit and explicit messaging. The ideology does have mechanisms through which its conception of progress emerges, namely through innovation. While this is not necessarily always the case, as there have been periods of history where technology has been lost, thus constituting a form of technological regression, the present institutional, technological, and cultural climate allows for technology to inevitably progress continuously. Technological progressivism also advocates in favor of novelty, as its focus is entirely upon the development of new technologies, based on the assumption that technological innovation is inevitably a positive development for humanity.

The problem with technological progressivism is that this assumption is not always correct. The ideology ignores the reality of evolutionary mismatch<ref>https://www.aporiamagazine.com/p/counterfeit-people-are-here-to-replace</ref>, whereby rapid novel changes to an organism’s environment, in this case a human’s, leads to a mismatch between an individual’s biology and their environment. In the context of human development, this can lead to maladaptive behaviors and responses in physical, social, reproductive, cognitive, emotional, or dietary domains. Furthermore, the transition towards certain technologies can create significant temporary or permanent social disruption which in some cases can potentially justify slowing down or halting technological progress in a particular domain.

There is nothing inherently wrong with having some form of progress as a political ideal, or using the term as a rhetorical device, in an attempt to advocate for a particular political, institutional, or cultural order. However, progress needs to be decoupled from the idea that it is inevitable and that it is intrinsically pro-novelty. Instead, progress should primarily be conceptualized as an improvement towards what is ultimately good. The term implicitly recognizes that there is a better state than what has existed previously, but it should make no pretensions that progress is synonymous with the passage of time or is inevitable. In reality, progress in whichever context it is discussed can only be inevitable with the presence of certain mechanisms which ensure that it is.

Progress also needs to be recognized under a universal frame, which does not merely apply to social policy or cultural norms in the case of social progressivism, or correlates of economic development in the case of technological progressivism. The expanding moral circle and technological development can certainly be seen as aspects of progress, when taken in moderation and with respect to more fundamental societal goals. However, these conceptions of progress lack sufficient generality to function as good frames through which to view progress.

A proper view of progress thus must be universal, and must recognize the institutional, cultural, and even biological mechanism by which it can be achieved. The core question which then arises is what the ideal ought to be according to which arrangements are to be viewed as progressive or regressive. To answer this question, I will first examine human anthropology, and the core causes and justifications for human civilization in the first place.

## On Metaethics and Social Anthropology

While the domain of areas which philosophy has reigned over and been taken seriously in has shrunken significantly over the past few millennia, ethics continues to be a hotly debated subject within the field. There has been little intellectual progress in the field, owing to the fact that there is still significant disagreement over the fundamentals of ethics, such as whether an ethical system which values the actions taken in themselves or the ends that those actions lead to is preferable. The answers to these questions have significant and far-reaching implications for how we organize society.

Because of the is-ought gap, the primary way in which ethical systems are tested against one another is through examining the consistency of said systems. This is done to demonstrate that an individual does not fully commit to their worldview, and as such, they must ultimately reject the worldview. The implicit assumption that morals must be extremely rigid and based on abstract systems largely ignores the context in which morality emerged. However, the core observation that moral systems ultimately derive from the underlying intuitions of people is correct.

This begs the question as to what the underlying intuitions of humanity are. In order to ascertain this, we must examine how morality emerged and why it even emerged in the first place.

Humans are a biological organism, which, like any other, evolved over the course of millions of years into what they are today. Like many primates, humans evolved in the context of social communities. We are not eusocial creatures whereby we become one big superorganism, but we are naturally social and evolved in contexts where we require being in communities in order to survive. Human communities evolved because without communities, humans died and were outcompeted by humans who were in communities<ref>https://dwz.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/114/2016/11/MoralBookReview.pdf</ref>. Many biologists argue that our brains increased in size in large part due to the necessity to survive and compete effectively within these communities, an idea commonly referred to as the “social brain hypothesis”<ref>https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19575315/</ref>.

In order for communities to function, there needs to be a set of norms which are established and generally adhered to<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Elephant-Brain-Hidden-Motives-Everyday/dp/0190495995</ref>, as otherwise there isn’t sufficient trust for the community to function properly. These norms can be referred to as morality or ethics, and are norms which can either be enforced socially or through the use of force. Humans evolved to adapt to the reality that social rules exist, which is why humans naturally have certain moral intuitions which present themselves cross-culturally, as social psychologist Jonathan Haidt has shown with his work on moral foundations<ref>https://moralfoundations.org/</ref>. While the particulars of moral systems certainly vary substantially across cultures, the fact that morality exists cross-culturally and we have evolved moral intuitions along a number of dimensions shows that morality in some sense is natural.

Furthermore, due to high intelligence and general social integration among humans, humans have a greater capacity and willingness to engage in actions which benefit others<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Sociobiology-New-Synthesis-Twenty-Fifth-Anniversary/dp/0674002350</ref>, not only to directly benefit themselves, but also in some cases benefit others at the expense of themselves. This phenomenon, generally referred to as kin selection<ref>https://www.britannica.com/science/Hamiltons-rule</ref>, is necessary across many animal species for the raising and survival of offspring to occur. However, in  humans, it regularly extends to a general sense of obligation to those who are more proximate genetically or relationally. This is why humans naturally feel a greater sense of obligation and belonging towards family and their own ethnic group, and also why friends tend to be more genetically similar to one another than to random people in the general population<ref>https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25024208/</ref>.

Thus, humans value their own survival, but are also willing to make some sacrifices to help the survival of others. This can help spread the genes of the individual further than a naive purely self-interested orientation and thus grant the individual greater opportunities to survive genetically. Humans primarily value their own survival, given that their own survival ensures the survival of the greatest proportion of their genome. This is partly contingent on them being in and maintaining their social groups, but in order for people to have an incentive to stay in a given social group and benefit properly from it, there needs to be a balance in place between the interests of the individual and the interests of the social group itself, or the collective.

Adaptive social systems are thus consequentialist in nature, as they are outcome-oriented towards survival interests. However, adaptive social systems are neither purely utilitarian or purely egoist, and recognize the nature of humans and how morality emerged. While other systems can in theory be devised for how morality is to occur, these systems will ultimately fail in any high-stakes context. Under any condition of harsh Darwinian natural selection, nature’s veto will eliminate other moral systems over time. This is exactly how the current moral system as described here emerged in the first place.

Throughout most of human history, people lived in small tribes of about 150 people or less, a number referred to as Dunbar’s number<ref>https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26189988/</ref>. This is an estimate based on an extrapolation of the relationship between primate social group size and relative neocortex size onto human social group sizes, and is in line with real-world observations of human friendship circles. The core limiting factor behind Dunbar’s number is the fact that the average person is only able to cognitively process every individual in a social group at up to a maximum of around 150. Beyond that number, people deindividuate from one another. This is why smaller communities and social groups tend to have greater cohesion and trust<ref>https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.05270</ref>, because as the number of individuals in a community gets larger, the ability for people to socially process everyone within the group diminishes significantly.

Once social groups exceed 150, many types of norm enforcement, such as the use of gossip, become significantly less effective, as people within the community become less and less socially recognizable to one another. As a result, such communities are lower in trust, have less social cohesion, have more conflict, and are more likely to split apart.

The way in which communities of a larger scale solve the problem of norm defection is through a greater use of non-social forms of enforcement, such as laws and hard power. This paves the way for the evolution of the state, which is said to hold a monopoly on the use of force. Communities which greatly exceed Dunbar’s number in size have the advantage of a greater ability for specialization and greater ability to exploit economies of scale in production. This makes such communities significantly stronger, and explains why over the course of recent human history, human populations have generally converged towards states.

Effective states require a general belief amongst the population that the state is morally justified, and also require certain concessions from individuals, such as taxation. Thus, individuals belong to states due to their advantages and due to their ability to survive. Nonetheless, a balance must still be struck between individual-level interests and the interests of the collective.

States are ultimately necessary for large societies due to human nature, and non-state based societies were ultimately undermined and outcompeted under nature’s veto in the same way that individuals in communities outcompeted individuals outside of communities. This is why purely anarchistic conceptions of societies ultimately fail.

This anthropological account of the history of social organization shows that morality is fundamentally consequentialistic in nature, as well as the reality that any proper view of progress must be grounded in the effective functioning of humans, both as individuals and as collectives. Under this view of progress, more progressive modes of social organization are those which benefit the ability for people to survive and thrive, not only now but into the future. A proper view of progress must account for the importance of the well-being of people today as well as the well-being of people tomorrow, such that societies are good when people cross-generationally are thriving, people are having children, there is general material prosperity, and people are well-socialized and well-integrated into communities or society. In short, it recognizes that people simultaneously exist as individuals as well as collectives, rather than merely being one or the other.

Any other view of progress risks getting outcompeted under competitive pressures. However, this risk of being outcompeted is not inevitable, and progress is thus not an inevitability under this conception. States as described in this text did not exist for the vast majority of 200,000 year lifespan of the human species. They emerged at a particular point in time, and required specific environmental and cognitive prerequisites to be fulfilled. It was only once those prerequisites were fulfilled that an evolution, or progression, in human social organization could occur. The challenge of any progressive view is thus not merely to define progress, but also to create the mechanisms under which progress can occur. The next section will outline these mechanisms in a more abstract manner.

## Progress and Evolutionary Processes

As discussed in the previous section, the state came about from an evolutionary process, as did human moral systems. Evolutionary processes are thus essential towards the achievement of progressive ends. This section will further detail how evolutionary processes work, as these are critical towards an understanding of how progress can be achieved.

Evolution fundamentally refers to change, and occurs along a wide range of dimensions, although it is traditionally studied and discussed in the context of the evolution of organisms. It should be noted that evolution is not necessarily good or bad. Evolution is to some degree inevitable, but we also have varying capacity to control it in its numerous dimensions to suit our needs. Technological progress, as previously shown, is generally done through evolutionary processes, although can lead to some harms for humanity. Thus, while evolution is very important for progressive processes, evolution in and of itself is morally neutral.

The most commonly discussed form of evolution, biological evolution, requires there to be variability in traits which are heritable in part or in whole, as well as selective mechanisms which favor some traits over others. Through this process, organisms which have traits which are selected against will gradually die off or be unable to reproduce, and will decrease in commonality in successive generations. Similarly, organisms which have traits which are selected for will gradually become more common in successive generations.

This process of evolution from a biological perspective can be generalized to other forms of evolution, with the two mechanisms abstracted further. Evolution, in general, requires variability, and a selection mechanism which favors some parts of that variability over others. This is how the evolution of social groups in humans emerged, which co-evolved alongside human genetic evolution, as gene-culture co-evolution is an inherent aspect to humanity. The evolution of social groups occurred in the way it did because there was variation in social structure or lack thereof, and selection mechanisms which led to differential survival and reproduction probabilities between individuals making up different social structures. This gradually led to some social structures being favored over others.

There are some who would object to the idea that these evolutionary processes, at least in a biological context, would apply to societies today. It is true that humans are so effective at survival that they can come up with a significant range of social organizations, all of which display relative stability over many generations<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Sociobiology-New-Synthesis-Twenty-Fifth-Anniversary/dp/0674002350</ref>. However, this does not mean that evolutionary processes are not occurring or that some social organizations are not more effective at achieving progress compared to others. Furthermore, research has shown that if anything, human evolution has sped up in pace over the past 5,000 years<ref>https://www.science.org/content/article/human-evolution-speeding</ref>, and research has continually shown that selection mechanisms have selected in favor of or against a large number of human traits within roughly that time span<ref>https://reich.hms.harvard.edu/sites/reich.hms.harvard.edu/files/inline-files/2026_Akbari_Nature_selection_0.pdf</ref>.

The reality that humans, cultures, and social organizations have co-evolved next to each other is something which is even more important today than it was in the past. Despite significant medical and economic advances making it so that virtually everyone can, in theory, survive and reproduce, virtually any study looking at the link between heritable traits, such as intelligence, and fertility suggests that in the long-run there will continue to be directional selection across a range of traits<ref>https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19323856/</ref>. Whether we like it or not, and whether or not we deliberately do anything, human populations will continuously biologically evolve in various ways.

Once again, the purpose of this is not to suggest that evolution in the ways it occurs is intrinsically good or bad, but that evolution is the fundamental process by which organisms are able to optimally adapt to their environment. As such, the process can be adapted and analogized to how societies can, in theory, optimize themselves, so long as the proper underlying conditions are met.

In contemporary society, evolutionary systems will require variability in inputs, which requires an open society, a notion popularized by philosopher Karl Popper. While the notion of an open society has been abused by some political actors to merely advance social progressivist causes<ref>https://web.archive.org/web/20150305202847/http://www.politico.com/magazine/story/2015/03/ferguson-inc-115765.html#.VYgzbYtFCUk</ref> – just as his concept of the “paradox of tolerance” has been misused to argue against freedom of speech, when it was never intended to do so<ref>https://quillette.com/2023/08/31/tolerating-intolerance-the-free-speech-paradox/</ref> – the concept broadly is a useful way to examine how progress occurs<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Open-Society-Its-Enemies-One/dp/0691158134</ref>.

Progress in a mechanistic sense, occurs in environments which are fundamentally open. This requires, first and foremost, the capacity for people to speak freely, debate, and implement ideas. Thus, precedence must be given to having freedom of speech, as well as economies with a strong private market sector to maximize innovation. Effective and functional societies require the presence of both openness as well as strong supporting institutions in place to ensure that progress can occur, thus explaining why the strongest economies tend to be characterized by mixed markets where there is often strong government involvement in areas such as research and development<ref>https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/new-economics-industrial-policy</ref>.

We will now return to our more detailed and comprehensive understanding of progress, expounding upon the ways progress should be understood and how it can occur mechanistically.

## Intellectual Progress as a Bedrock

The understanding of progress as outlined here, whereby progress is consequentialistically oriented towards human flourishing, first and foremost requires an intellectual foundation. This intellectual foundation must come up with a full understanding of what is entailed by progress, in addition to an understanding of what social, cultural, and institutional norms are necessary for the fulfillment of progress.

A common but erroneous assumption made about political disagreement is that all differences in viewpoint are a function of differences in values. This is an assumption generally borne out of intellectual laziness, as it allows people to shirk the often long and difficult progress of finding out what is true about the world, and gives people a sense of unearned self-righteousness and superiority for merely having the most morally “correct” beliefs.

This erroneous assumption about political disagreement is in part made because of the over-emphasis which is commonly placed on broad moral abstractions, such as the tripartite concepts of liberty, equality, and fraternity, which have informed and led to the creation of foundational political ideologies. Ideologies such as libertarianism and anarcho-capitalism place emphasis on liberty; socialism and social progressivism on equality; and conservatism and nationalism on fraternity.

However, it is not difficult to imagine instances where all three of these values ought to be shirked to some degree, even if they can be useful considerations in certain contexts. As shown previously, some violations of liberty are necessary for the formation and sustainment of states, which require strong legal systems in place<ref>https://www.fraserinstitute.org/sites/default/files/economic-principles-for-prosperity.pdf</ref>, which in turn necessitate some involuntary taxation. Similarly, societies have certain mechanisms in place to ensure that more competent governance is achieved, at the expense of equality, such as through the institution of a minimum voting age which creates legal and structural inequalities in voting rights. Finally, there are many cases where excessively ingroup-oriented policies, or policies which excessively promote fraternity, can lead to slower economic development, as in the case of economic nationalism. Research consistently shows that a generally free trade regime promotes economic growth<ref>https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/660841468162283031/pdf/775730JRN020080alization0and0Growth.pdf</ref>, something which requires a less parochial attitude towards social and political organization.

These ideological abstractions should largely be ignored in favor of pragmatic functionality, or what directly promotes flourishing. Research consistently shows that ideology impairs the ability for individuals to engage in sound reasoning<ref>https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1948550619829059</ref><ref>https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13546783.2022.2038268</ref>, suggesting that strong attachment to abstractions leads to an impairment in people’s rationality. There is also some evidence suggesting that many of the neurobiological correlates of religious belief and political belief are similar<ref>https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5180221/</ref><ref>https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7195862/</ref>, which helps confirm the idea that political beliefs are generally not rationally derived.

Ideologies are largely constructions from decades, and in many cases, centuries ago. Virtually all scientific fields will reject many mainstream views in their fields from 50 or more years ago, merely because scientific knowledge can progress significantly in fields, even over the course of a single decade. Should we expect that our knowledge about the world world has increased significantly, and yet that it wouldn’t speak to at least modifying ideologies as they existed decades ago?

Rather than appealing to ideological abstractions, there needs to be a robust epistemology through which we can view the ways in which human flourishing is promoted. This must come, first and foremost, from a recognition that ideological disagreements exist heavily on the basis of differences in understanding of descriptive facts, rather than differences in values. In reality, if it was the case that centrally planned economies led to greater economic growth and more prosperity for everyone, however defined, that descriptive fact, even if it did not determine whether centrally planned economies were desirable, would certainly have a bearing on the question relative to if that descriptive fact was not the case.

The misconception that political differences are primarily a question of values, borne out of intellectual laziness, is heavily perpetuated by contemporary academic institutions and the cultural milieu which follows from it. These have led to the normalization of substituting any substantive argumentation in favor of the discrediting of viewpoints through other means, most notably through various accusations of “bigotry”.

Most policy questions are in large part or in whole reducible to empirical questions about what the effects of the policies would be, as is with many questions about institutions, technology, culture, and other fundamental aspects of society. It is thus crucial to develop a coherent and effective epistemology through which truth can be arrived at, as well as to understand the institutional, cultural, and even potentially biological means through which the production of knowledge can be maximized. This latter issue can be arrived at through this new epistemology.

Contemporary academic institutions have created a false dichotomy between idealism and materialism to analyze society. This leads to the assumption that the only means by which societal change can happen is through ideas, or material or environmental forces. But there is a third option, which is to understand the influence which biology and evolutionary processes have, an option which can be referred to as biorealism. There must be an integration of idealism, materialism, and biorealism to come to effective and comprehensive explanations of social phenomena, and thus understand how we should govern and structure society, culture, and institutions.

A robust epistemology also requires a stronger and more sophisticated approach to studying social phenomena which moves away from purely verbal accounts of knowledge and phenomena which risk cultural and personal bias, in favor of the use of quantitative research and a strong empiricist approach. Contemporary academic institutions have become closed systems due to their dominance by social progressivist forces, which promote ideological hegemony, as well as hostility and censoriousness towards ideas which may undermine the empirical presuppositions upon which social progressivism is based. Academia has been consistently shown to ideologically lean left in the social sciences, and contrary to the notion that this is due to social progressivism merely being more rational or social progressives being more truth-oriented, in reality this bias is largely due to a hostile climate for those who question the ideological hegemony, active discrimination against non-leftists, and subtle biases which present themselves throughout academia<ref>https://www.adamsmith.org/research/lackademia-why-do-academics-lean-left</ref><ref>https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/qpn57_v1</ref>.

Knowledge production requires diverse viewpoints and open systems in order to be maximized. Research consistently shows that intellectual diversity, as measured by diversity in educational background<ref>https://docs.iza.org/dp6973.pdf</ref> or values<ref>https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13504851.2015.1130785#abstract</ref>, is positively related to productivity and innovation, while the types of diversity commonly promoted by social progressives, such as racial or diversity, appears to have no effect on innovation if it is not accompanied by intellectual diversity<ref>https://www.emerald.com/ejim/article-pdf/25/6/941/1864210/ejim-09-2021-0474.pdf</ref>. Furthermore, research into how researchers choose to analyze a given empirical topic with political implications suggests that they will come to vastly different and even opposing conclusions, even when using identical data<ref>https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2203150119</ref>. It is only when there is diversity in research approaches that we can uncover this inherent ambiguity and uncertainty of knowledge, and then hope to make scientific progress. Just as biological evolution requires genetic variation, evolution in knowledge first and foremost requires variance in intellectual approaches, values, and theoretical outlooks.

In order for viewpoint diversity to occur, there must be the capacity to even express diverse viewpoints in the first place. The capacity to express differing viewpoints requires strong protections for freedom of speech as well as academic freedom. Research consistently shows that freedom of speech is positively related to economic growth and innovation<ref>https://justitia-int.org/analyse-ytringsfrihed-skal-nytte/</ref>, and that academic freedom is positively related to innovation<ref>https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4384419</ref>. This provides empirical support for the idea that an evolutionary view of knowledge production, as protected through legal and social infrastructure, is crucial towards progress.

Thus, there must be the ability for people to say controversial things, or things which may go against the prevailing norms or mores of society, and strong protections for those who do. These strong protections must not merely be limited to legal protection, but also cultural and institutional protections to ensure that people functionally speaking have the ability to speak freely. If a person is unfairly discriminated against in hiring for their views, is fired, is prevented from using major modes of communication such as social media, or mobbed by the general public for their views, functionally speaking freedom of speech is being threatened<ref>https://zerocontradictions.net/pdfs/freedom-of-speech.pdf</ref>.

Open systems require not only freedom of speech, which allows for variation in thought and ideas, but also generalized economic freedom, which allows for variation in the execution of ideas. Economic freedom, which relates to free markets, property rights protections, private ownership, low regulation, among other things, is generally associated with greater economic growth<ref>https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejpoleco.2005.06.003</ref>, as well as generalized prosperity along a range of metrics<ref>https://doi.org/10.1111/coep.12010</ref>. Markets must be supported by a mixed system whereby there is a strong public research and development sector and reasonable regulations such as patent protections which allow for innovation to be maximized<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Encyclopedia-Political-Economy-2-set/dp/041524188X</ref>.

Free markets allow for a large variety of intellectual innovations to be tested out in marketplace settings with relatively few barriers. Meanwhile, a public research and development sector creates avenues for innovations which may be too risky for private firms or individuals to be willing to invest in, too expensive, or without a clear and immediate expected profit return. Contrary to assumptions that public investment into research and development will crowd out private sources of innovation, public investment may actually crowd in private investment and thus lead to more innovation even in the private sector<ref>https://d1bcsfjk95uj19.cloudfront.net/files/Where_do_innovations_come_from.pdf</ref>.

Technological development has been consistently identified as the fundamental driver of economic growth within societies<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Encyclopedia-Political-Economy-2-set/dp/041524188X</ref>. While as discussed previously, technological progressivism has limitations in that technology can create problems just as it can solve them, it is the case that material prosperity in a general sense is positively related to human flourishing. Thus, while technological development should never be seen as an end in itself, as a means to an end it can be extremely valuable.

A general free market approach does not preclude the possibility of a social safety net, or of some industries having more government control. Universal healthcare systems, which generally necessitate a mixed market structure to some degree, have been identified as performing stronger than healthcare systems characterized by non-universality, such as that of the United States<ref>https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/fund-reports/2024/sep/mirror-mirror-2024</ref>, and this stronger performance on health outcomes can translate into higher economic growth due to a positive relationship between population health and economic growth. Thus, despite the general positive relationship between economic freedom and economic growth, this does not preclude some reforms away from a purely laissez-faire system and in favor of a mixed market system. Our understanding of progress must always keep consequentialism in mind, not be dogmatically attached to rigid understandings of policy, and be empiricist in terms of approaches to designing institutions and policies.

Intellectual progress is not the end goal of progress. However, it ultimately provides the foundation upon which future innovations are to happen. Open societies allow for the exchange of ideas, which allows for ideas to be discussed which may progressively improve upon society. In addition, open societies allow for healthy market economic structures for ideas to be implemented and tested in real-world settings, thus allowing for further progress to occur. Intellectual progress is thus perhaps the most important dimension of progress, and the one which has unfortunately been ignored the most, or even undermined in contemporary culture.

While thus far, the discussion has examined the importance of intellectual progress, its origins, and some of its institutional requirements, it has thus far sidestepped what is perhaps one of the most important cultural and institutional values to be upheld: competence. Progressive institutions must recognize the importance of institutional norms and laws which promote competence where it is most needed and best employed, and recognize that competence is biologically unequally distributed. Progress thus necessitates a degree of inequality, a topic we will now turn to.

## Progress and Inequality

Competence, or the ability to perform a task effectively or efficiently, is the fundamental dimension upon which individual differences in the ability to engage in intellectual progress, as well as the ability to enact the fruits of intellectual progress, can be assessed. Progress, intellectually and elsewhere, depends in part upon the proper application of competence, whereby everyone fulfils niches they are most specialized in, and whereby those with the highest competence are put in positions of highest importance. Progress is thus fundamentally meritocratic in nature.

Another way in which progressive systems must thus be open is in terms of there being no rigid class boundaries. Income and social classes must be fluid, and people should be able to rise and fall insofar as they demonstrate competence or lack of competence respectively. Progressive systems must promote class mobility insofar as it is reflective of competence, and market systems appear effective in promoting this. Research has shown that intelligence, the prime way in which competence is measured, is related with class mobility, in that those with low intelligence show downward class mobility, while those with high intelligence show upward class mobility<ref>https://www.cremieux.xyz/p/intelligence-and-social-mobility</ref>.

But what is intelligence? Intelligence is best understood according to the g factor, or the generalized factor of intelligence<ref>https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15651931/</ref>. This refers to the fact that individuals who score highly in one intellectual domain tend to also score highly in other intellectual domains. General intelligence is highly heritable, with research typically pointing to a heritability of general intelligence of 0.8, suggesting that 80 percent of the variation in general intelligence between individuals is a function of variance in genetics<ref>https://www.sebjenseb.net/p/we-now-have-280-pairs-of-identical</ref>.

Intelligence is associated with a wide range of outcomes. But most importantly, it has been shown to be one of the best, if not the best, predictor of a person’s education, occupation, and income<ref>https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0160289606001127</ref>. Contrary to environmentarian theories of social mobility, intelligence is a stronger predictor of these factors than parental socioeconomic status is. Intelligence is also important on a group level, as it has been identified as one of the strongest predictors of economic growth on a national level as well<ref>https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S016028961630318X</ref>. Intelligence is thus not merely important for individuals, but also for society more broadly.

Due to the high heritability of intelligence, people differ in their genetic propensity for intelligence. This means that not everyone will be able to have the same ability in any intellectual domain as other people will. All research looking at human accomplishment shows that a very small proportion of individuals make the vast majority of contributions in any given field. For example, analysis of citation numbers across papers suggests that only a very small proportion of scientists meaningfully contribute to scientific progress<ref>https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.178.4059.368</ref>. Similarly, even though national average intelligence is a strong predictor of economic growth, the intelligence of the smartest fraction of individuals in a society is an even stronger predictor of economic growth than simply looking at average national intelligence<ref>https://www.researchgate.net/publication/366691469_Smart_Fraction_Theory_A_Comprehensive_Re-evaluation</ref>. This indicates that the cognitive elite in any society is disproportionately responsible and critical for its economic development.

The goal of open societies and open systems should be to ensure that exceptional people, regardless of their starting position in life, are able to be put in positions where they can make exceptional contributions. This requires not merely a society which legally promotes meritocracy, but also a cultural acceptance that inequality is not inherently bad, and that inequality on the basis of intelligence is inevitable.

One of the largest damages which social progressivism has done to human flourishing is to produce excessive resentment over inequalities which are recalcitrant, good, or natural, between individuals, and particularly between groups. In reality, inequalities in intelligence which lead to differing social and economic outcomes are not only inevitable between individuals, but they are also inevitable between groups. There is some evidence which suggests that men have greater variability than women in cognitive ability, despite relatively similar averages between the sexes, suggesting that any field which requires a high intelligence will select for more men than women. Similarly, research has consistently identified racial differences in intelligence, which appear to be of substantial genetic origin<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Know-Debunking-Myths-about-Intelligence/dp/1108493343</ref>. These group differences suggest that in fields which require a high cognitive ability, Jews, East Asians, and whites will almost inevitably be overrepresented relative to other racial groups.

This does not mean that there ought to be arbitrary discrimination against women or people who belong to lower-scoring racial groups. Every individual should be judged on their own merits, and there should be legal measures in place to ensure that discrimination does not happen. However, these anti-discrimination measures, and our general culture, should recognize that disparities in outcomes do not necessarily mean discrimination. Civil rights legislation which makes this erroneous assumption, as exists in the United States and elsewhere<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Origins-Woke-Corporate-Identity-Politics/dp/0063237210</ref>, should be scrapped in favor of a regime which favors nondiscrimination but recognizes the inevitability of unequal outcomes.

In addition, none of this should be used to suggest that individuals who are of low intelligence should be dehumanized, impoverished, or otherwise mistreated by society. A meritocratic social order which promotes competence should ultimately be used to uplift everyone in the society. In fact, an individual with an IQ of 85 would ultimately be better off in a society with an average IQ of 115 rather than a society with an average IQ of 70. This is because the more intelligent society has a greater capacity to support and uplift everyone within the society, due to its greater capacity for innovation, economic development, and ultimately progress.

Furthermore, as has been previously discussed, there should be a social safety net for people who struggle to succeed in society. Research shows that inequality, when coupled with poverty or a general lack of economic well-being on an absolute level, is related to greater social resentment and hence leads to negative externalities, such as higher crime<ref>https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1468-4446.12083</ref>. Thus, it is not only beneficial to the potential recipients of social spending to have a social safety net, but also to the broader society as a whole.

Contemporary social progressivism has attempted to flatten hierarchies and promote individuals with lower competence into positions of higher prestige and importance. This has been one of the most damaging aspects of the ideology, as it significantly undermines progress while claiming to advance it. True progress necessitates the promotion of merit and competence, and necessitates a recognition that some level of inequality will be inevitable as a result. Such a view of progress will benefit everyone, irrespective of their level of competence, or their socioeconomic standing in society.

The promotion of competence is one of the most important means by which intellectual progress is promoted, but it must always be remembered that intellectual progress is not to be pursued for its own sake. Intellectual progress is the foundation upon which the promotion of human flourishing can occur, and intellectual progress does this through allowing us to understand what institutional, cultural, and ultimately, societal changes are necessary for its promotion. This issue is where we will turn to next.

## Institutional and Cultural Progress

Institutions and culture are the primary environmental vectors through which human flourishing is achieved. Institutions effectively refer to anything which shapes and constrains behavior, while culture refers to, as discussed by E. O. Wilson, “environmental tracking devices,” which can include group-level beliefs, rituals, religions, and related practices<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/Sociobiology-New-Synthesis-Twenty-Fifth-Anniversary/dp/0674002350</ref>. Institutions are necessary to govern human interactions, and as previously discussed, played an invaluable role in the development of civilization as human communities grew to a size large enough that informal social norms could no longer effectively govern human interactions. Culture is similarly necessary, as it provides individuals with group cohesion as well as a sense of purpose. Culture allows individuals to relate to and interact with one another in a society, and as such is crucial to the identities of many, whether explicitly stated or not.

Whereas intellectual progress is much more abstract in its formulation, with it being oriented towards an end which is quite disconnected from itself, in the case of institutions and cultures, these ends are much more clear. Institutions and cultures which are progressive are those which ultimately promote human flourishing.

There are two core principles which underlie progress in the contexts of institutions and cultures. Firstly, institutions and cultures should be experimental and diverse in nature. This once again requires an open society which allows for diversity in speech and expression. However, these are not the only prerequisites.

The primary prerequisite for experimentation and diversity in cultures is the protection of freedom of association. While discrimination or exclusion must be prohibited legally and socially in the public realm, which concerns direct economic transactions, some level of exclusion in the public realm – the realm of the home, community life, and interpersonal relations – is a necessary element to protect cultural diversity. There must be cultural and political measures to allow people to create cultural associations, or even neighborhoods dedicated to preserving a particular culture, and these measures must not give preferential treatment or grants to particular groups over others. There ultimately needs to be equality under the law with regards to the protection of multiculturalism within societies.

This legal protection allows for social cohesion to be sustained within communities, because such communities have defined boundaries and characteristics, without sacrificing the general economic benefits associated with integration in the public realm. It also allows for a much larger number of cultural permutations to thrive and experiment with, as the implicit tendency for cultures and peoples to merge together when placed in social proximity to one another is abated. This leads to the development of data and knowledge, allowing us to understand which cultural permutations work to favor human flourishing and which do not.

The ability for cultural groups to segment should they choose to also allows for less conflict between individuals in cases where groups may otherwise have conflict with one another. There are many instances within areas such as education where cultural groups have significantly diverging views over what ought to occur, in ways which deal with deep cultural values over issues such as the relationship between children and their family. When cultural groups are given protections to segment from one another, this allows for people to freely move into communities where their education system and other institutions reflect their cultural preferences, which can ultimately reduce conflict and hardship which otherwise might exist.

Beyond the existence of biological differences which naturally lead to socioeconomic inequalities, environmental differences can also do so. It is well-known that culture can be a significant contributor to social and economic outcomes of individuals. For example, research has consistently shown that countries which are more culturally individualistic tend to experience higher economic growth rates than countries which are more culturally collectivistic<ref>https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/does-culture-affect-long-run-growth</ref>. Policy which preserves or promotes cultural diversity will thus inevitably lead to inequalities between individuals and groups – a tradeoff between diversity and equity. This is generally not properly integrated into social progressivist thought, which leads to significant incoherence in their policy prescriptions.

My view is that there is a balance to be struck between institutions trying to accommodate difference versus erasing differences. The accommodation of differences generates greater social cohesion and greater experimentation, at the expense of innovation and growth in other realms such as technology. Meanwhile, the erasure of differences can generate significant economic gains for a society when done effectively. The best way to attempt to preserve both outcomes is to recognize that the gains from the accommodation of differences are primarily to be found in the private realm, while the gains from the erasure of differences are primarily to be found in the private realm. Thus, there ought to be accommodation in the private realm, and erasure in the public realm.

From the perspective of institutions themselves, experimentation should exist in all forms, and there should be no strong stigma against particular institutional permutations. Perhaps one of the greatest developments within empirical social sciences in recent decades has been the so-called “credibility revolution” in econometrics<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credibility_revolution</ref>. This shift has been primarily characterized by a move towards causal analysis in macro-level areas where experimentation was historically viewed as impossible or untenable. Since the credibility revolution, there has been a marked rise in the use of natural experiments within economics, although unfortunately passing by other fields such as sociology<ref>https://statsandsociety.substack.com/p/the-credibility-revolution-is-passing</ref>. Natural experiments refer to situations in which the conditions necessary for an experiment to normally be conducted – namely, a control group and an experimental group – are organically arrived at through exogenous changes to only one of two comparable groups. This has led to a significant increase in the certainty social scientists can have about the causal effects of particular phenomena.

This type of experimentation should be done consciously by policymakers, as it allows for a greater understanding of which institutional and political arrangements are best to promote human flourishing. One area where experimentation can be particularly useful is with democratic institutions, or institutions concerned with how governance is done. Such institutions have generally not evolved at the same pace that progress has been made in other domains, such as technology. While markets are typically considered effective ways to organize goods and services, as previously discussed mixed markets are ultimately preferable, and there is no reason to dogmatically hold to market-based systems if they are shown to not be effective in achieving this end. Similarly, there should not be a dogma in favor of fully democratic institutions, whatever such a thing would even mean, if they are not shown to be as effective in ensuring effective societal progress and governance.

While democracy has traditionally been said to be “by the people, for the people”, if the two conflict, we should generally support having a state which is governed for the people, rather than a state which is governed by the people. We must always remember that consequences are what matter in governance, and inputs only matter insofar as the outputs they produce. Countries such as Singapore have shown that a country can have relatively undemocratic institutions while also having effective governance and quality of life, and there is some evidence to suggest that many countries in the west are at a level of democracy beyond what is optimal for maximizing economic growth<ref>https://www.amazon.ca/10-Less-Democracy-Should-Elites/dp/1503603571</ref>.

The second principle with regards to institutional and cultural progress, which must be understood, is something which has been mentioned previously, but should be discussed in more detail here. It is that progress is not intrinsically tied to time. Systems which are old can be progressive, and new systems can be anti-progressive. Producing positive feedback loops which lead to progressive improvements can be more difficult in the context of culture relative to other areas, at least in the short run. In the long run, societal growth can gradually lead to pressures for culturally lagging societies to catch up to others, even if not by direct threat then by envy. As a result, we should not expect that indexes of developments in culture according to time would be a useful indicator of their progressiveness, as there can be significant noise, particularly in the short-run.

A significant tradeoff which presents itself in the context of open societies, particularly with regards to culture, is between progress and cohesion. Greater progress happens when there is greater room for experimentation and the capacity for cultures to change, even rapidly change. While that can be great for improving cultures in the long-run, in the short-run it can destroy social cohesion, as the undermining of cultures can cause substantial harm to many people.

One thing which must always be remembered is that while progressive systems in theory offer continual self-improvement, which implies change, that does not mean that change is intrinsically good. Especially in the context of culture, there are often good reasons for why certain norms exist as they do. They are oftentimes long-standing, tried-and-true ways for people to relate with one another in communities, which creates significant value which was only arrived at through evolutionary processes themselves. Attempts to deconstruct many cultures can ultimately be damaging if not carefully considered, and not merely damaging to social cohesion but also for broad prosperity in other dimensions.

As stated previously, gene-culture co-evolution is a very common phenomenon which has become incredibly important over the past 5,000 years or so as evolution and technological progress have accelerated. Thus, while there can be experimentation in areas such as culture, in the case of culture especially, it should generally be done at smaller scales and through piecemeal reforms, recognizing that older systems may exist for a reason a-la Chesterton’s fence<ref>https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._K._Chesterton#Chesterton's_fence</ref>.

An excellent example of this is the presence of gender, which gives people a strong sense of purpose and meaning, even if it can constrain behavior in unfair ways sometimes. Monogamy has been a particularly effective social norm which generally involves complementary gender roles to at least a fluid degree, providing cohesion, societal stability, and assisting with the effective raising of children. Monogamous societies have generally been more economically progressive, because the family is the bedrock of a strong society<ref>https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rstb/article-abstract/367/1589/657/21759/The-puzzle-of-monogamous-marriageReview-Puzzling?redirectedFrom=fulltext</ref><ref>https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-021-04301-7</ref><ref>https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0022002719859636?__cf_chl_f_tk=IAouTCtYpSJh1qeygWaSkzNQmuMhukdGnhaY6YJ8tqo-1782962954-1.0.1.1-QqzBLTKy_8cuUK.LwFjrvfz4JGEWWoZ42K4f3nn5bF8</ref>. Cultural and technological developments which have undermined it, such as those associated with the sexual revolution, as well as the rise of no-fault divorce, have ultimately led to an undermining of familial norms, which harms children and societal prosperity more broadly.

Attempts to question something as fundamental as gender roles should always be done with care, and if one wants to try alternative modalities it should generally be done at a small scale, and if possible, in areas where children are not directly involved. There are thus tradeoffs and conflicts which necessarily present themselves with regards to institutional and cultural progress. However, this is similar to the reality of technology. While technology has been disruptive and harmful, that does not mean that we shouldn’t encourage technological progress and innovation. Furthermore, it does not mean that such progress does not come with significant benefits. After all, gene-culture co-evolution implies that cultures have evolved significantly, and more recent cultural arrangements can in many cases be invaluable in producing high-quality economic and social outcomes compared to cultural arrangements of previous eras.

We thus must have cultural diversity to some degree, even if it comes at the expense of social cohesion temporarily, as that is how progress is to occur. Nonetheless, efforts should be taken to ensure that it does as little harm to others as possible, through ensuring that ambitious cultural and institutional reforms are piecemeal, small-scale, and non-disruptive to the broader society until they have been shown to work, allowing for freedom of association in the process.

## Biological Progress in Contemporary Society

I have previously mentioned that the common dichotomy made between idealism and materialism as the only mechanisms by which societal change can be explained is a false one. We have thus far discussed intellectual progress, which encompasses an idealistic way of viewing the world, as well as technological, institutional, and cultural progress, which encompasses a materialistic way of viewing the world. However, we have not discussed biological progress, which encompasses a biorealistic way of viewing the world.

While not much attention is placed on the genetics or biology of people in popular discourse, it is clear, as has been discussed extensively throughout this text, that genetics and biology matter. They can determine social outcomes for individuals and groups, they affect how well humans can adapt to certain environments, and they can significantly affect people’s well-being. For example, in a world in which technologies exist that undermine social interaction, such a world would be relatively less progressive if humans were naturally more social animals, and relatively more progressive if humans were not. Interactions between people’s biology and their environment are crucial to understand when discussing how progress is to occur.

Just because one does not care about genetics does not mean that genetics does not care about us. Humans have been continuously evolving, and these patterns can be positive or negative depending upon the traits which are selected for. In pre-industrial societies, infant mortality was extremely high. This meant that selection mechanisms primarily concerned the ability to survive. However, with the rise of medical technologies which caused infant mortality to plummet, and for virtually all humans born to reach maturity, the primary selection mechanism by which change can occur is with differential reproduction. Whether people are able and willing to pair up and reproduce with one another, and who ultimately does, is what will predict future genetic evolution of human populations.

While significant debates can be had over what should or should not be done regarding this reality, it seems clear that people would care about what traits this differential fertility selects for. For example, if it organically ends up selecting in favor of intelligence, such an outcome would be preferable to one where it organically ends up selecting against intelligence, ceteris paribus. So is there any genetic selection currently going on? Research done into polygenic scores for various traits suggests that genetic traits which predict higher cognitive ability are being selected against, while traits which reflect high time preference behaviors such as ADHD are being selected for<ref>https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361797770_Human_Capital_Mediates_Natural_Selection_in_Contemporary_Humans</ref>. This means that future generations will likely be more impulsive, less willing to save in the long-run, which is very bad for economic growth, may be more criminal, and will be less innovative. The effects of these selection mechanisms are often shown to be relatively small<ref>https://www.cspicenter.com/p/are-we-getting-dumber</ref>. However, with them compounding over time, they could have substantial deleterious consequences, ultimately rendering this an anti-progressive trend.

This opens the question as to what degree attempts to engineer reproductive outcomes are justified or not. From a policy perspective, it should be noted that present policies do have non-neutral effects on reproductive outcomes, either intentionally or unintentionally. Intentionally, this happens through laws against incest, which are generally justified on the basis that incestuous relations lead to the creation of children with severe genetic defects. Unintentionally, this happens through allowing and in some cases even subsidizing birth control and abortions. Given that virtually any study done on relative use of birth control shows that some people are more likely to use it than others, the genetic effects of such policies will not be neutral. It seems that, at least in extreme cases, legal restrictions on reproduction can be justified.

It should be noted that even if deleterious genetic changes were not occurring, it still would not be the case that attempts to improve the genetic “quality” of people would be bad. This is because there could be opportunity costs associated with not doing it, or gains which are being ignored. In general, biological progress in the form of affecting population genetics should be libertine in nature, and should primarily be focused upon educating people about genetics and the importance of making good decisions, as well as providing access to reproductive technologies which can empower people to make such decisions.

In the coming decades, it is likely that we will be able to directly change people’s genetics through wider access to embryo selection, a technology which is already entering markets<ref>https://www.npr.org/2026/05/06/nx-s1-5704317/genetic-embryo-screening</ref>, as well as genetic engineering more broadly. Open access to this will help to solve problems of dysgenics, and open the door for direct biological progress to occur. However, even in the absence of direct genetic changes, environmental changes to people's biology can be beneficial through the expansion of medical technology. Such technology has significantly reduced infant mortality, and while that has come with challenges, it is still ultimately a progressive development.

With both types of biological progress, there will undoubtedly be issues which will arise, including a type of “genetic” mismatch, which can refer to mismatch between a person’s biology and their external environment when the biology was not arrived at through direct adaptation with the environment, or when there is mismatch between various aspects of an organism. These are risks associated with both exogenous, environmental changes to biological aspects of a person, as well as with genetic engineering technology, and there should be serious conversations about to what degree such interventions can be justified, including with cosmetic surgeries, surgeries aimed to express more extreme gendered aspects in one direction or another, and general transhumanist modifications.

However, the net long-run benefits will significantly exceed these costs as long as they are carefully considered. These technologies have the potential to in the future significantly improve traits of future generations, such as intelligence, which will generate positive externalities to society everywhere, improving competence, innovation, and the quality of institutions and cultures.

The core challenge with some aspects of biological progress is that, on the surface, it appears difficult to ethically and practically justify measures to promote it. However, in reality, many of these measures are already implemented explicitly or implicitly, and there are many libertine means by which these types of changes can be done, which would ultimately be beneficial for society without imposing unfair restrictions on individuals.
